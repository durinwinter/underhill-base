6                                         INTRODUCTION
 7   This standard specifies general concepts for the automated integration of intelligent equipment,
 8   called Process Equipment Assemblies (PEAs), in higher-level automation systems, called
 9   Process Orchestration Layers (POL) and provides the basic definitions for the specification of
10   interfaces, information models and workflows. The term of “process” in Process Equipment
11   Assembly and Process Orchestration Layer refers to all kind of processes independent of any
12   concrete industry, e.g., process industry, logistics and manufacturing industry.

13   These concepts can be used for the automation of modular production systems as well as for
14   the integration of automated units into conventional plants (package units). The focus of this
15   specification is on the automation of modular production systems in different industries.
16   Driving forces behind this progress are…
17          … enhancing the efficiency due to reuse and reutilizing not only engineering efforts but
18           as well physical equipment,
19          … increasing flexibility and adaptability of         production systems due to the
20           exchangeability of equipment and
21          … reducing time-to-market due to the ability to combine pre-automated, pre-tested and
22           possibly pre-qualified equipment.
23   The engineering workflow of modular production systems considers both the automation
24   technology of the Process Equipment Assemblies, called PEA engineering, and their integration
25   into a Process Orchestration Layer, referred to as POL engineering. This series of standards
26   defines and specifies the Module Type Package (MTP), which semantically describes the
27   interfaces and their functions of the automation technology of PEAs thus enabling the
28   integration of the PEAs into a POL.
29


30   This standard part focuses on the following aspects:

31          Concept of Automation Services, encapsulating the procedural control of automated
32           functions

33          Concept of Process Values, enabling PEA-spanning base automation functionality using
34           contiuous exchanged information

35          Definitions and descriptions of the MTP ServiceSet

36          Definitions and description of the MTP ProcessValueSet

37          Supplementing definitions and descriptions for the MTP TextSet

38          Optional Concept of Complex Types, which allows the usage of array- and structure-
39           oriented information in defined DataAssembly definitions

43   1   Scope

44   This document specifies the fundamental concepts for Automation Services and Process
45   Values. Both are key building blocks for the concept of distributed automation functionality.
46   Whereas the Automation Services represent a concept for a state-based procedural
47   encapsulation the Process Values represent information connectors for base automation
48   functions like closed-loop controls and interlocks between multiple Automation Services and
49   PEAs.

50   Both concepts consist of standardized interfaces, which ensure the interoperability between
51   multiple vendors, and model definitions, which extend the standardized interfaces with the
52   required semantical information, to make it automatically importable.

53   The specified interfaces were primarily defined for the needs of pharmaceutical or special
54   chemistry solutions. With the new scope of MTP to all kind of automated processes some
55   interfaces contain functionality and possibilities which are not needed in some industries. Thus,
56   there will be a Best Practice Whitepaper for Complexity Reduction describing approaches to
57   systematically reduce complexity and the number of variables of the DataAssembly definitions
58   specified in this document.

103   Operator Station Level

106   5.3
107   Procedure
108   Specific execution variant of an Automation Service

109   5.4
110   Process Value
111   Incoming and outgoing Process Values are connectors between Automation Services to
112   implement connections of base automation functions

113   5.5
114   Report Value
115   Dedicated information used for reporting of an executed Automation Service

116   5.6
117   Required Equipment
118   Equipment required for the execution of a Procedure of an Automation Service

120   Service Interaction

122   Process Orchestration Layer

123   5.8
124   Service Parameter
125   Parameter used for configuration and parameterization of Automation Services and their
126   Procedures

127   5.9
128   Service Position

160   7   Concept for Automation Services and Process Values

161   The MTP concept introduces a Service-based concept for encapsulation of automated
162   functionality. To distinguish OT-related services as specified in this standard from IT-related
163   services, the term Automation Services may be used for the former. In this standard, the terms
164   Automation Service and Service are used synonymously and refer to OT-related services.

165   The Service concept represents a state-based procedural control which is similar to the
166   specified concepts within the ISA 88 [1]. Furthermore, a broker-based cross communication via
167   the POL is specified for connecting cross-equipment base automation functionality like closed
168   control loops and interlocks.

169   A chemistry PEA “Stirring Reactor”, for example, could thus offer the Service “Stirring”. Since
170   the reactants are to be filled into the reactor, the reactor also offers the Service “Filling”, which
171   can differ depending on the number and designation of the filling nozzles, e.g., “FillingA” and
172   “FillingB”. If the reactor has a heating system, the “Heating” Service can also be implemented.
173   A corresponding parameter set of this Service could be the target temperature, the temperature
174   rise rate, or the dwell time.

175   A logistics PEA “Form-Fill-Seal”, for example, could offer a machine Service “Packaging”, which
176   provides several Procedures for continuous packing or packaging of a defined number of bags.

177   Within the Orchestration recipe, a sequence of those Services is called in a defined order which
178   coordinates the Service to produce a concrete product. The Service described in the MTP form
179   the basis for the recipe creation, which generally takes place before the Modular Plant is
180   commissioned. Several Service of a PEA can be used simultaneously. For example, in a
181   continuously operated reaction process, the start-up of the reactor can take place in parallel
182   with the preparation of the reactants.

183   The Service concept follows a fixed and non-configurable state machine similar to the concept
184   of IEC 61512-1 [2]. The Orchestration sends commands to the PEA to change the state of a
185   Service. Within each state, various programs, e.g., processes according to IEC 61131-3 [3], are
186   implemented. The programs within the states then control the control modules necessary for
187   the automated function and evaluate the corresponding signals from the sensors. Different
188   states of a Service may contain the same functionality, for example, stopping (in the Stopping
189   state) and aborting (in the Aborting state). The current state of each Service is reported by the
190   PEA to the POL.

191   In summary, this standard stipulates the necessary specification parts for orchestrating
192   automated functions via the state-based procedural control and the broker-based cross-
193   communication for PEA-crossing base automation functions. As shown in Figure 1, PEAs
194   communicate with the POL via defined interfaces. All relevant data of the PEA are transmitted
195   to the POL.

205   8.2     Concept of the Automation Service

206   The general concept of Automation Services is based on a state-based procedural control with
207   the possibility of several variants of a functionality, called Procedures, and corresponding
208   parameters, Report Values and required equipment.

210   8.2.1     General Definitions

211   8.2.1.1     Access Channels

212   Within the interfaces defined in this standard, access paths are defined that enable the
213   operation of a Service by an HMI system, by the PEA-internal automation or by an automated
214   system of the POL. The operation is performed by control signals, which trigger a switching
215   action within the DataAssembly, and value specifications for the transmission of a value. The
216   following naming conventions are used to distinguish between these interfaces.

217   Automatic (Aut)
218   Automatic variables (Aut) are used as read-only variables to make PEA-internal (automated)

226   switching actions or the transfer of value specifications from an automated system of the POL.
227   Those variables end with the extension “Ext”.

228   Internal (Int)
229   Internal variables (Int) are used as read-only variables to make PEA-internal value
230   specifications visible. Those variables end with the extension “Int”.

256   8.2.2    Service Mode Concept

257   The operation mode of Services is controlled via the ServiceMode.

258   Each Service has a state machine, as described in Section 8.2.3 in which, depending on the
259   ServiceMode, state transitions may be requested either by the operator, the superordinate
260   process control (POL) or another Service in the PEA (PEA-internal logic). The following
261   concepts are used to switch between these possible sources (POL, PEA-internal logic or
262   operator) for a Service:

263   ModeControl is used to switch between the ServiceModes Offline, Operator, and Automatic.

264   SourceSelection is used in the “Automatic” ServiceMode to switch between Automatic Internal
265   and Automatic External.

266   In the Offline ServiceMode, the Service is not ready for operation. When leaving the Offline
267   ServiceMode, the Service transfers the subordinate actuators used by the Service to the
268   Automatic ServiceMode (request for switching without feedback). When changing the
269   ServiceMode back to Offline, it is up to the PEA manufacturer whether an automatic change of
270   the ServiceMode of the subordinate control elements takes place.

271   In Operator ServiceMode, the Service is operated by the operator via the POL (OSLevel > 0)
272   or a local panel/display (OSLevel = 0) via the Operator variables (Op).


      © Copyright PNO, NAMUR, ZVEI 2024 – All Rights Reserved                      Page 21 of 107 Pages

      Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values               Version d0.9

273   In the Automatic-External ServiceMode, the state transitions that are not triggered automatically
274   are operated by an automated system of the POL. In the Automatic-Internal ServiceMode, all
275   state transitions are triggered internally in the PEA.

276   A Service shall only be taken into the Offline ServiceMode in the Idle state. When changing
277   from the Offline ServiceMode to another ServiceMode, the Idle state shall be actived. Switching
278   between Operator, Automatic Internal, and Automatic-External shall be possible in any state.
279   The change of ServiceMode does not cause a change of state.

287   The State variables implement a state machine with three possible ServiceModes: Offline,
288   Operator, and Automatic (as depicted in Figure 2). Transitions between any two modes are

307   External state) the request shall be ignored by the PEA and no change of the source mode shall

335   8.2.3     State Machine

336   8.2.3.1     Structure of the State Machine

337   The state machine of each Service consists of 16 states in five levels and is specified in
338   accordance with [1]. The state machine consists of transient and non-transient states. All states
339   ending with “-ing” are transient states. Execute is transient if it is a self-completing Procedure
340   of the Service (see Section 8.2.4.1). The transient states are reached by commands (request
341   of a state transition) and change automatically to the subsequent state after completion of the
342   function implemented in them. This sequence of self-termination is deviated from, if a command
343   occurs that leads to a higher level of the state machine, whereas level 5 is the highest level.

344   Level 1 contains the states Execute, Pausing, Paused, and Resuming.

345   Level 2 contains the states Starting, Completing, and Unholding as well as level 1.

346   Level 3 consists of the states Holding and Held as well as level 2.

347   Level 4 contains the states Stopping and Stopped as well as level 3.

348   Level 5 contains the states Idle, Completed, Resetting, Aborting, and Aborted as well as level 4.

349   The levels describe that a change from a lower level to a higher level can take place at any
350   time. The change to level 2 takes place via the Complete command, the transition to level 3
351   takes place via the Hold command, the transition to level 4 takes place via the Stop command,
352   the transition to level 5 takes place via the Abort command. In Figure 3, the commands are
353   indicated by the arrows. The state transition “SC” means “State Change” and indicates that this
354   transition is triggered by the upstream state. This type of state transition is used exclusively
355   after transient states. The state transitions (commands) that can be requested by the POL are:
356   Start, Complete (only for continuous Procedures, see Section 8.2.4.2), Reset, Pause, Resume,
357   Hold, Unhold, Stop, Abort, and Restart. Figure 3 shows the state machine. The POL is informed
358   about the current state of any Service by the PEA. The state model is not configurable, i.e., it
359   shall be fully implemented for each Service (states and state transitions), but states may be
360   empty.

363   8.2.3.2    Semantics of States and Transitions

364   In order to be able to control the Services accordingly, the semantics of the individual states
365   are defined. The central loop for the execution of a Service consists of the states Idle, Starting,
366   Execute, Completing, Completed, Resetting. The semantics of the individual states are defined
367   as follows:

368   Idle: Idle is the non-transient initial state of the Service in which all information technology
369   conditions for starting the Service are fulfilled. No functionality in the sense of the process-
370   technical function is executed yet.

371   Starting: The transient state Starting describes the start-up of the Service towards Execute. In
372   Starting, the necessary actuators are started, and all actuators are set to the initial position for
373   Execute.

374   Execute: A Service in the Execute state executes the procedural function(s) of the Service. This
375   means that in the Execute state, the main functionality is implemented from a process-technical
376   point of view, e.g., a “Mixing” Service in Execute executes the “Mixing” function, which consists
377   of mixing several reactants. The corresponding actuators, e.g., valves and drives, are activated
378   for this.

379   Completing: The transient state Completing describes the planned shutdown of a Service. If the
380   functionality in Execute has been completed or, in the case of continuous Procedures, the
381   termination of the functionality has been requested by the operator or the Orchestration, a
382   shutdown functionality is executed, and all actuators are set to the default position for Idle.

383   Completed: In the non-transient state Completed, the Service has completed all functionalities,
384   and no further functionalities are executed.

385   Resetting: In the transient state Resetting, all conditions for the Idle state are restored. The
386   Reset command shall not be requested by the service itself, but only by the operator, the POL,
387   or another Service of the PEA.

      © Copyright PNO, NAMUR, ZVEI 2024 – All Rights Reserved                       Page 26 of 107 Pages
                    Draft for Member Review. Do not Claim Conformance!
      Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values              Version d0.9

388   The remaining states can be combined into the following further loops:

389   Pause loop: The Pause loop, consisting of the states Pausing, Paused, and Resuming, is used
390   to implement the logic that is executed when a pause of the Service has been requested. The
391   Pause loop is requested by the Pause command and shall be exited by the Resume command,
392   unless a higher-level command is triggered. The transient states Pausing and Resuming serve
393   to prepare or end the non-transient state Paused.

394   Hold loop: The Hold loop, consisting of the states Holding, Held, and Unholding, is used to
395   implement the logic to be executed to allow for safe intervention to avoid damage or unintended
396   production loss. Those conditions that are relevant for the service lead to entry into the Hold
397   loop. The Hold loop is requested by the Hold command and shall be exited by the Unhold
398   command, unless a higher-level command is triggered. The transient states Holding and
399   Unholding serve to prepare or end the non-transient state Held.

400   Stop loop: The Stop loop, consisting of the states Stopping and Stopped, is used to implement
401   the stopping of the service functionality. Stopping the service may result in damage to the
402   product. It shall not result in damage to the PEA. The Stop loop can be triggered by the POL,
403   the operator, or also by the PEA. The transient state Stopping is used to prepare the non-
404   transient state Stopped. The Stopped state is exited by the Reset command, unless the
405   command Abort is triggered.
406
407   Abort loop: The Abort loop, consisting of the states Aborting and Aborted, is used to implement
408   a quick shutdown of the service. This may cause damage to the product and the PEA. The
409   Abort loop can be triggered by the POL, the operator or also by the PEA. The transient Aborting
410   state is used to prepare the non-transient Aborted state. The Aborted state is exited by the
411   Reset command.

412   The transition between the states is done either as an automatic state change or with defined
413   commands which trigger the transitions. The commands are defined as follows:

414   Reset: The reset command is used to trigger a transition from the non-transient states of level
415   3, level 4 or level 5 to the transient state Resetting to prepare for the next execution.

416   Start: The Start command triggers the transition from Idle to the transient state Starting which
417   is followed by the transition to Execute.

418   Stop: The Stop command triggers a transition from each state in level 3 to the transient state
419   Stopping which is followed by the transition to Stopped.

420   Hold: The Hold command triggers a transition from each state in level 2 to the transient state
421   Holding which is followed by the transition to Held.

422   Unhold: The Unhold command triggers the transition from Held to the transient state Unholding
423   which is followed by the transition to Execute.

424   Pause: The Pause command triggers a transition from Execute to the transient state Pausing
425   which is followed by the transition to Paused.

426   Resume: The Resume command triggers a transition from Paused to the transient state
427   Resuming which is followed by the transition to Execute.

428   Abort: The Abort command triggers a transition from each state in level 4 to the transient state
429   Aborting which is followed by the transition to Aborted.



      © Copyright PNO, NAMUR, ZVEI 2024 – All Rights Reserved                    Page 27 of 107 Pages
                   Draft for Member Review. Do not Claim Conformance!
      Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values               Version d0.9

430   Restart: The Restart command triggers a transition from Execute to the transient state Starting
431   which is followed by the transition back to Execute.

432   Complete: The Complete command triggers a transition from each state in level 1 to the
433   transient state Completing which is followed by the transition to Completed.

434   When designing the Services, it shall be ensured that it is possible to change from all states of
435   Level 2 into the Hold loop, but that the loop always returns to Execute. In cases when the
436   complete Services functionality is not to be executed after a return to Execute from the Hold
437   loop, it shall first be checked in the Execute state whether a state transition to Pausing or
438   Completing is to be triggered by the PEA.

452   8.2.3.4         State Control

453   Due to the different access channels and the ServiceMode concept the following three
454   command variables are available for controlling a Service:

455   CommandOp is intended for manual use from a human-machine interface or by the operator.

456   CommandExt is intended for use from an Orchestration system within the POL.

457   CommandInt is intended for PEA-internal use.

458   Depending on the ServiceMode, the PEA selects the corresponding channel.

459   The variables are implemented as control words. Each bit of the control word thereby stands
460   for a command. Only one bit of the control word shall be set at a time. The corresponding
461   encoding can be found in Section 8.2.3.3.

462   The logic in the PEA resets the command in the control words CommandOp and CommandExt
463   (status bits = 0) as soon as it has interpreted it. This prevents a command from being
464   permanently pending and unintentionally being executed several times. It prevents, for
465   example, a Service from being started, completed or reset several times if the commands Start,
466   Complete, and Reset have been sent and remain pending on the part of the POL. If more than
467   one bit of the control word is set, the control word is ignored by the PEA and set to “0”.

468   8.2.3.5    State Feedback

469   The Service reports back its current state and the possible transitions by the following variables:

470   StateCur indicates the current state of the Service. The status word StateCur is coded as the
471   State column defined in Table 3. States outside the definition are invalid. In the ServiceMode
472   Operator and Automatic, only exactly one state shall be active. Active means that the bit of the
473   variable is set to TRUE, all other bits shall be FALSE.

474   CommandEn indicates the current triggerable state transitions via the command variables. It
475   is set dynamically by the PEA. It is encoded as defined in the CommandEnable column of
476   Table 3. Thereby a value of TRUE means that a control command is possible and a value of
477   FALSE means that a setting of this command is prevented. Several bits may be set at the same
478   time. Exceptions are defined for Stop and Abort as exceptional states. The control command
479   Stop shall not be prevented in any of the states of levels 1, 2, and 3. The control command
480   Abort shall not be prevented in any of the states of levels 1, 2, 3, and 4.

481   Whether a Service can be started or restarted also depends on the values to be applied for its
482   Service Parameters. The rules on how the enable information for the Start and Restart
483   command shall be calculated by the PEA are specified in Section 8.2.5.4.4.

484   8.2.4     Procedures

485   In order to make the Services of the PEAs as efficient and flexible as possible, Procedures are
486   introduced. Each Service shall have at least one Procedure. A Procedure is an execution
487   variant of the Service. Procedure parameters, Process Values, and Report Values that are
488   necessary for the operation of the execution variant of the Service shall be assigned to the
489   individual Procedure. Process Values, Report Values, or Procedure parameters may be shared
490   across multiple Procedures, but Report Values and Procedure parameters only within the same
491   service. In this case, all of them reference the same DataAssembly by setting the same value
492   for the RefID attribute.

493   Furthermore, Procedures are differentiated into self-completing and continuous Procedures.
494   Different Procedures within a Service are allowed. The POL shall support Services with
495   different types of Procedures. Within a PEA, Services may be designed in such a way that they
496   have only one Procedure. The information whether a Procedure is self-completing or continuous
497   is described in the MTP. Each Procedure shall display its current health status for diagnostic
498   purposes as well as its command info. The variable CommandInfo indicates if the commands
499   Start or Restart are allowed at the Procedure the CommandInfo belongs to, at the moment. The
500   CommandInfo variables of all Procedures of the Service shall be updated by the PEA in the
501   ServiceModes Automatic and Operator. The CommandInfo is encoded equally to the
502   CommandEn variable defined in Section 8.2.3.5.

503   A Service shall only be able to operate in one Procedure at a time; simultaneous operation of
504   a Service in different Procedures shall not be possible. To start a service, a Procedure shall
505   be selected.

506   8.2.4.1    Self-Completing Procedures

507   In a self-completing Procedure, the state change “SC” for the planned termination of the Service
508   (state change from Execute to Completing) is triggered by the Service itself. The POL or the
509   operator can track the state transition via the states Completing and Completed. Termination
510   of the Service by PEA-external access shall only be possible by means of the state transitions
511   Stop and Abort.

512   8.2.4.2    Continuous Procedures

513   For continuous Procedures, the Complete command to terminate the Service is sent to the PEA
514   by the POL or the operator depending on the ServiceMode. The possibility to complete a
515   continuous Procedure is indicated by the corresponding bit of the CommandEn variable.

516   8.2.4.3    Procedure Control

517   Procedures are selected via the Service interface. Each Procedure has a Service-wide unique
518   ID (ProcedureID). This is used to identify the Procedure and is used to select the correct
519   Procedure during operation (or when starting a Service). A Service shall not have a Procedure
520   with ProcedureID = 0. When a Service is initialized (i.e. leaves the Offline mode), the
521   ProcedureOp/ ProcedureExt/ ProcedureInt/ ProcedureReq/ ProcedureCur shall be set to 0 by
522   the PEA. The value 0 means that currently no Procedure has been selected and thus no
523   Procedure can be started.

524   To select the Procedure the desired ProcedureID shall be written by using one of the following
525   three variables, which are selected according to the ServiceMode:

526   ProcedureOp shall be used by the human-machine interface within the POL.

527   ProcedureExt shall be used by the Orchestration system within the POL.

528   ProcedureInt shall be used internally in the PEA.

529   Depending on the current ServiceMode the PEA selects the corresponding channel.

530   8.2.4.4    Procedure Feedback

531   In addition to the control words and the variables for setting the Procedure, the Service reports
532   back its current status. This is done via the following variables:

533   ProcedureCur contains the ProcedureID of the currently active Procedure. This changes when
534   a Procedure is started and thus the Procedure is applied by the Service. A start is possible both
535   from Idle to Starting, via Start, and from Execute to Starting, via Restart.

536   ProcedureReq shows the ProcedureID of the currently requested Procedure, which will be
537   started at the next Start/Restart. The POL can use this variable to check whether a requested
538   Procedure has been applied by the PEA and which Procedure will be executed when the Service
539   receives a Start/Restart command.

540   Only valid ProcedureIDs defined for a service are included in ProcedureReq. If an invalid
541   ProcedureID or ProcedureID = 0 is written to one of the Procedure control variables, the
542   ProcedureReq shall be set to 0 to indicate its invalidity.

543   When the Service enters the Starting state, ProcedureReq is transferred to ProcedureCur and
544   the PEA logic resets ProcedureOp and ProcedureExt to 0.

545   A start is possible both from Idle to Starting, via Start, and from Execute to Starting, via Restart.
546   ProcedureCur contains the ProcedureID of the currently active Procedure or 0 if no Procedure
547   is active. ProcedureCur changes when a Procedure is started and thus the Procedure is taken
548   over by the Service.

550   RequiredEquipment describes any DataAssembly of an actuator, sensor, or software entity that

555   8.2.5      Service Parameters

556   8.2.5.1      Types of Service Parameters

557   Services can be specified in more detail by parameters. Furthermore, parameters may be
558   necessary that specify the execution of the Service in more detail. Each Service Parameter is
559   assigned to exactly one Service. The concept foresees two kinds of parameters.

560   Configuration parameters are used to set basic settings for a Service before it is executed.
561   That’s why configuration parameters are assigned to the Service itself and not to any
562   Procedure. Typically, they effect the Service as a whole.

563   Procedure parameters are used to parameterize variants of functionality of the Service, e.g. to
564   set setpoints. That’s why, Procedure parameters are assigned to certain Procedures which need
565   this individual information.

566   Configuration parameters and Procedure parameters can be defined in four different data types:

567            binary value
568            integer values
569            analogue values
570            string values
571   Both parameter types are described in more detail in the next sub sections.

572   8.2.5.2      Configuration Parameters

573   Configuration parameters are used to make basic settings for a Service. Any configuration
574   parameter always belongs to exactly one Service. Configuration parameters cannot be assigned
575   to individual Procedures. New values for configuration parameters should be set before the
576   execution of a Service is started, i.e., in the Idle state of the Service. Nevertheless, if the PEA
577   indicates that new values for configuration parameters can be set, the values may be updated
578   in other states, too.

579   Configuration parameters are used, for example, to select sensors to be used as input values,
580   set limits of upstream and downstream PEAs or parameters for calculating inerting cycles.

581   8.2.5.3      Procedure Parameters

582   Procedure parameters are Procedure-relevant. They are assigned to one or more Procedures.
583   New values for Procedure parameters should be transmitted from the POL to a Service before
584   the Service is started from the Idle state (Start) or restarted from the Execute state (Restart).
585   When entering the Starting state, the PEA shall apply the new values for the Procedure
586   parameters. In addition, if the PEA indicates that new values for Procedure parameters can be
587   set, new values may be transmitted and applied in other states, too.

588   Procedure parameters are used, for example, to specify recipe-relevant setpoints (temperature
589   specifications, flow specifications, pressure specifications) or controller parameters (gain, hold
590   time).

591   8.2.5.4     Parameter Value Assignment

592   8.2.5.4.1     Concept of Parameter Value Assignment

593   The following mechanisms shall be supported to implement a controlled value assignment for
594   Configuration parameters and Procedure parameters. The process to set new values consists
595   of two steps.

596   In a first step, the new value is transmitted to the PEA. The PEA shall check if the value can
597   be used, e.g., if it is within the value range. The value, which will be used, is signaled by the
598   PEA via the VReq variable. This variable may be read by the POL to check if the value
599   assignment has been prepared successfully.

600   In the second step, the new value is to be applied. The PEA shall indicate if a new value can
601   be applied to prevent a value assignment during times where the PEA does not accept new
602   values, because this might lead to unsafe or undefined states. The ApplyEnable variables are
603   used to indicate if new values can be applied.

604   The controlled value assignment means to the POL to apply the prepared values at precisely
605   defined times or to synchronize a value assignment (e.g., with other value assignments). The
606   application of new values is triggered by the Apply command.

607   In addition to the Apply command, prepared values for Procedure parameters assigned to a
608   Procedure are applied when the respective Procedure is started or restarted.

609   The controlled value assignment shall be possible in the different ServiceModes, which means
610   that the Apply command exists for the ServiceModes Operator, Automatic Internal, and
611   Automatic External.

612   If a prepared value has been applied successfully, the PEA shall set the variable VOut to the
613   new, currently active value.

614   Apply for Operator (ApplyOp)
615   ApplyOp variables (ApplyOp) enable the triggering of a controlled value assignment by the
616   operator. ApplyOp variables end with the name extension “ApplyOp”.

617   Apply for Automatic-Intern (ApplyInt)
618   ApplyInt variables (ApplyInt) enable the triggering of a controlled value assignment by a higher-
619   level Service within the PEA. ApplyInt variables end with the name extension “ApplyInt”.

620   Apply for Automatic-Extern (ApplyExt)
621   ApplyExt variables (ApplyExt) enable the POL to trigger a controlled value assignment.
622   ApplyExt variables end with the name extension “ApplyExt”.

623   ApplyEnable (ApplyEn)
624   The signaling of readiness for value assignment to the POL is done within the interface via
625   ApplyEnable variables. ApplyEnable variables end with the name extension “ApplyEn”.

626   8.2.5.4.2     Apply at Parameter Level

629   second step the value is applied. Any assignment of a new value requires an enablement by
630   the PEA signaled by the ApplyEnable variable of the corresponding parameter. If the enable
631   signal is available, the value transfer can be triggered via the Apply variable of the active

677   8.2.6       Report Values

678   A Report Value is a value that is made available by a Service for the verification and
679   documentation obligations of a system operator. Report Values are assigned to individual
680   Procedures of exactly one Service. A Report Value can be continuously updated within the
681   assigned Procedures.

682   To be able to read a consistent set of all Report Values, the concept of Report Value freezing
683   is used. Whenever the Report Values of a service are frozen, their continuous update is halted,

      © Copyright PNO, NAMUR, ZVEI 2024 – All Rights Reserved                   Page 35 of 107 Pages
                     Draft for Member Review. Do not Claim Conformance!
      Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values                Version d0.9

684   and the timestamp is set to the last update. Report Value freezing always addresses all Report
685   Values of a service.

686   Report Value freezing is initiated by two distinct mechanisms.

687   The explicit Report Value freeze is triggered by the POL via enabling the ReportValueFreeze
688   on a service. When ReportValueFreeze is disabled, the explicit freeze is disabled as well.

689   The implicit Report Value freeze is conducted by the PEA itself. Whenever a service enters one
690   of the states of Completed, Stopped or Aborted, its Report Values are frozen. This implicit
691   freeze does not interfere with the explicit freeze mechanism, i.e. ReportValueFreeze is not
692   changed. When the service enters Resetting, the implicit freeze is disabled again.

693   If the Report Values were frozen at the time of a state change to one of the states Completed,
694   Aborted, and Stopped, their values are updated and provided with the time stamp of the state
695   transition.

696   Report Values can optionally signal via a MissedValue flag whether their value has changed at
697   least twice during the frozen state. The MissedValue flag thus indicates that a value could not
698   be read by the superordinate system. Whether a Report Value supports this functionality is
699   modelled in the MTP by means of the RC MissedValueFlag.

710   unequal to 0. The value of the variable points to the text to be displayed in the associated list.

976    9.2     Concept of the Automation Service

977    The general concept of Automation Services, defined in Section 8.2, introduces a state-based
978    procedural control with the possibility of several variants of a functionality, called Procedures,
979    and corresponding Service Parameters, Report Values and required equipment.

980    The following sections specify extending DataAssembly definitions for the use of complex types
981    within Service Parameters.

982    9.2.1     AttributeTypes for Complex Data Types

983    Some industries are working with information which is organized in structures and arrays. To
984    enable those industries to benefit from MTP concept as well, this sub section introduces how
985    complex data types are modeled.

986    The foundation of this is already defined in MTP Part 1 introducing the modelling of complex
987    data types as AttributeType in AutomationML for the use within method items. The same
988    modelling rules apply here as well.

989    The used complex data types shall be defined in the ATL MTPComplexTypesATLib. This library
990    contains a list of all used complex data type definitions inside the MTP.

991    The defined complex data types of the MTPComplexTypesATLib will be later used in a Type
992    variable within each of the DataAssembly definitions. The Type variable indicates the shape of
993    the concrete complex data type. This variable shall be set to the AttributeType which is behind
994    each of the dynamically linked variables of the DataAssembly definitions. For structured data
995    the Type variable shall be derived from the AT StructuredDataType. For array data the Type
996    variable shall be derived from the AT BaseDataType. The AT StructuredDataType and AT
997    BaseDataType are already defined in MTP Part 1. The Type variable is used as suffixes of a
998    structured variable within a concrete DataAssembly definition.

 999   Protocol-specific specifications are defined in Part 5 / 5.x which indicate if they support complex
1000   types.

1001   9.2.2     Multiplexing of Array Types for Services

1002   There are some use cases where PEAs provide lists of parameters or configurations. But in
1003   most cases only one element out of that array information is relevant. That’s why this
1004   BaseFunction defines a multiplexing mechanism using indexing to focus only on the indexed
1005   element of the array.


       © Copyright PNO, NAMUR, ZVEI 2024 – All Rights Reserved                       Page 76 of 107 Pages
                     Draft for Member Review. Do not Claim Conformance!
       Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values                   Version d0.9

1006   The mechanism specifies a list of six variables, shown in Table 35, IndexOp, IndexInt, IndexExt,
1007   IndexMin, IndexMax and IndexCur. All six variables are defined as DINT data type. IndexMin
1008   and IndexMax provide the lower and upper index boundaries of the indexed information.
1009   IndexOp, IndexInt and IndexExt represents the indexing of the multiplexing mechanism.
1010   IndexOp and IndexExt are writable and allow the access from external system to the
1011   multiplexing mechanism. Depending on the current ServiceMode the IndexOp, IndexInt or
1012   IndextExt are relevant. IndexCur shows the currently selected index. All variables are used as
1013   suffixes of an array datum within a concrete DataAssembly definition.

1014   The apply mechanism of the ParameterElement doesn’t affect the index settings.

1017   9.3     Overview of Aspect Definitions

1018   The DataAssembly definitions in this profile extend the root definition of the ParameterElement

1049   10.2     Concept of Cross-PEA Process Value interconnections

1050   10.2.1     Functionality of Process Value Interconnection

1051   For the operation of production systems, it can be necessary that a PEA requires data from
1052   other PEAs or must provide such data for other PEAs. Such data can be, for example,
1053   temperatures or position indicators. Process Values are used for this purpose. They come into
1054   use, for example, to implement interlocks or controls across PEAs (setpoint from PEA A, control
1055   and actuator in PEA B). Process Values shall be exchanged continuously.

1056   10.2.2     POL-based Exchange Mechanism

1057   For the continuous exchange of Process Values they are cyclically read and written from the
1058   POL from and to the PEAs. A read Process Value is provided by a PEA for external access. A
1059   written Process Value is written to a PEA by the POL.

1060   Process Values provide or expect values regardless of the ServiceMode or current state of a
1061   Service. The POL reads the Process Values from PEA A and can use them system-wide and
1062   across PEAs. The POL performs transformations, if applicable, to adapt Process Values to the
1063   possible range of values that the receiving PEA B can process as well. The POL then writes
1064   these values to PEA B to enable processing in the PEA.

1065   Since the physical limits at the sensor in the providing PEA and the measurement chain limit
1066   the absolute maximum value range of the Process Value, its unit and the limits of the value
1067   range shall be provided when transmitting the values.

1068   Also, the receiving PEA can only process certain units and value ranges. Therefore, both the
1069   physical unit and the limits of the values shall be configured on the receiving PEA in order to
1070   ensure correct transmission of the values. The PEA may allow for changing the default
1071   configuration as described in Section 10.2.5. For this purpose, a corresponding interface is
1072   defined for incoming Process Values.

1073   A distinction is made between ProcessValueOut for the provision of Process Values by a PEA
1074   and ProcessValueIn for the processing of incoming Process Values.

1075   Different data types are available for Process Value processing:

1076      binary value
1077      integer values
1078      analogue values
1079      strings

                      Draft for Member Review. Do not Claim Conformance!
       Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values              Version d0.9

1080   Only elements from the ProcessValueSet are to be used for the interconnection of PEA-wide
1081   functions, as other elements do not guarantee a continuous provision of values. Process Values
1082   are explicitly modeled in the MTP in the ProcessValueSet.

1083   10.2.3   Process Values Relations to Procedures of Automation Services

1084   Process Values are often processed in direct relation with Procedures. However, there are also
1085   cases where an assignment is not possible or doesn’t make sense.

1086   The following rules shall be applied for the assignment of Process Values to Procedures of
1087   Services:

1088      Measured values that can in principle be interconnected across PEAs shall be explicitly
1089       modeled as ProcessValueOut.
1090      Process Values supplied by a single control assigned to any Procedure shall be assigned
1091       to the corresponding Procedure.
1092      If a ProcessValueOut is supplied by a single control that is not assigned to any Procedure,
1093       then the ProcessValueOut shall also not be assigned to a Procedure.
1094      A ProcessValueIn, which acts directly on a Procedure, shall be assigned to the
1095       corresponding Procedure.
1096      A ProcessValueIn acting on a single control assigned to a Procedure shall be assigned to
1097       the corresponding Procedure.
1098      A ProcessValueIn for which the above rules do not apply shall not be assigned to any
1099       Procedure.

1100   If a Process Value is related to Procedures of a Service, an instance of the SUC ProcessValueIn
1101   or the SUC ProcessValueOut shall be assigned to the Procedure. The identical RefID thus
1102   establishes the model-technical relationship between the interface definition, derived from the
1103   InputElement or IndicatorElement, with the model definition in the ProcessValueSet and the
1104   Procedure.

1105   10.2.4   Implementation of Outgoing Process Values

1106   If a PEA provides Process Values for further processing in POL or other PEAs, this is done
1107   via the SUC ProcessValueOut, based on the IndicatorElements from MTP Part 3.

1108   As defined in MTP Part 3, meta information and a QualityCode are available in addition to the
1109   actual value. This code indicates the validity of the Process Value.

1110   For outgoing Process Values (ProcessValueOut), the interface definitions of the
1111   IndicatorElement or its derivatives are used. Thus, for outgoing Process Values the interface
1112   definitions BinView, BinMon, DIntView, DIntMon, AnaView, AnaMon, or StringView shall be
1113   used.

1114   10.2.5   Implementation of Incoming Process Values

1115   If a PEA requires Process Values from the POL or other PEAs for internal processing, these
1116   are transmitted to the PEA as ProcessValueIn. The actual value (V) and the associated
1117   ValueQualityCode (VQC) are written by the POL. The valid value range for analog and integer
1118   values may be configurable by the POL via the interface of the Ext variable during
1119   commissioning.

1120   In the MTP of the PEA, a list of the possible physical units with the corresponding limit values
1121   may be provided for each Process Value. This is done by means of a list of attributes of the AT
1122   InputElementConfig specified in Section 10.5.7.

1123   The following steps shall be carried out to change the default configuration:

1124      1) Write of UnitExt by the POL. This sets the absolute limits of SclMinExt/SclMaxExt
1125         predefined in the MTP.
1126      2) Write the absolute limits SclMinExt/SclMaxExt by the POL.
1127         These shall be within the absolute limits predefined in the MTP.
1128      3) If the PEA can basically accept new values and process the unit present at UnitExt, the
1129         PEA shall signal this via ApplyEn.
1130      4) By setting the Apply flag, the new unit and scaling settings are applied.
1131          i)   If the PEA can process the Process Values in the unit set at UnitExt and within the
1132               limits SclMinExt/SclMaxExt, it writes this feedback to the Cur variables.
1133          ii) If the PEA cannot apply the limits, the PEA limits the values to those described in
1134              the MTP. Thus, the POL can read out the current configuration for the units and the
1135              scaling from the PEA at any time.
1136      5) If the value (V ) is outside the set limits SclMinCur/SclMaxCur, the PEA limits the value
1137         to SclMinCur/SclMaxCur.

1138   For incoming Process Values (ProcessValueIn), the interface definitions introduced in the
1139   following section are used. All new interfaces BinProcessValueIn, DIntProcessValueIn,
1140   AnaProcessValueIn, and StringProcessValueIn are derived from the interface family
1141   InputElement.

1142   With the help of the AT InputElementConfig defined meta-information about the configuration
1143   options can be added to an InputElement. This AT describes the possible configuration variants
1144   of a variable below the attributes UnitExt, SclMinExt, SclMaxExt of an AnaProcessValueIn as a
1145   DIntProcessValueIn.

1146   The list of possible configurations is modelled in accordance with an OrderedList which
1147   represents a key-value pair list. This means that a child attribute shall contain a unique number
1148   in the Name and the corresponding value in the Value. The number of child attributes shall be
1149   the same for the three attributes mentioned. All child attributes of the three named attributes
1150   with the same key represent a consistent configuration record. The first key shall be 0.

1279   11.2     Concept of Cross-PEA Process Value interconnections

1280   11.2.1     AttributeTypes for Complex Data Types

1281   Some industries are working with information which is organized in structures and arrays. To
1282   enable those industries to benefit from MTP concept as well, this sub section introduces how
1283   complex data types are modeled. The foundation of this is already defined in MTP Part 5
1284   introducing the modelling of complex data types as AttributeType in AutomationML. The same
1285   modelling rules apply here as well.

1286   Complex Data Types are differentiated into Array and Structures. StructuredDataTypes can be
1287   defined using PrimitiveDataType or EnumeratedDataType. StructuredDataTypes can’t be
1288   nested.   The   defined    StructuredDataTypes      shall    be    added    into  the  ATL
1289   MTPComplexTypesATLib. Arrays can be used for PrimitiveDataTypes, EnumeratedDataTypes
1290   and StructuredDataTypes. Arrays do not have a dedicated definition inside the
1291   ATLComplexTypesATLib because they use only PrimitiveDataTypes or StructuredDataTypes.

1292   The PrimitiveDataType or the defined StructuredDataTypes out of the MTPComplexTypesATLib
1293   shall be used in a VType Variable within each of the DataAssembly using complex type
1294   definitions. The VType variable indicates the shape of the concrete complex data type. This
1295   variable shall be set to the AttributeType which is behind each of the linked variables of the
1296   DataAssembly definitions. For structured data the VType shall derive from the AT
1297   StructuredDataType. For array data the VType shall be from the AT PrimitiveDataType,
1298   EnumeratedDataType or StructuredDataType. Those ATs are defined in MTP Part 5.

1299   Within Protocol-specific specifications (MTP Part 5.X) it is indicated if the communication
1300   technology supports complex types.

1301   11.2.2     Multiplexing for Process Values

1302   For the Process Value interconnection of array-based information a multiplexing mechanism is
1303   used as well. At a certain point of time only one array element is accessible. The Process Value
1304   output side represents the information provider, and the Process Value input side represents
1305   the information consumer.

1306   There are cases where such array information is shared, and a consuming PEA shall be able
1307   to fetch the data from a providing PEA via the POL. For those cases there are ProcessValueOut
1308   and ProcessValueIn definitions given. The multiplexing information is transferred from the
1309   output side to the input side. Thereby, the indexed information shall be exchanged and not the
1310   whole array.

       © Copyright PNO, NAMUR, ZVEI 2024 – All Rights Reserved                    Page 99 of 107 Pages
                      Draft for Member Review. Do not Claim Conformance!
       Module Type Package – Part 4: Automation Services and Process Values                           Version d0.9

1311   When using multiplexing Process Values this leads to the limit that only point-to-point Process
1312   Value interconnections are possible (no connection from one output to multiple inputs). The
1313   reason for this is that a bidirectional information flow is necessary between Process Value
1314   output and Process Value input.

1315   The mechanism specifies a list of four variables, shown in Table 54, IndexSel, IndexMin,
1316   IndexMax and IndexCur. All four variables are of DINT data type. IndexMin and IndexMax
1317   provide the lower and upper index boundaries of the indexed information. IndexSel represents
1318   the indexing of the multiplexing mechanism and is writable and allows the access from the
1319   interconnected Process Value. IndexCur shows the currently selected index. All variables are
1320   used as suffixes of an array data within a concrete DataAssembly definition. If a value is
1321   selected outside of the allowed range the requested index is limited to the boundaries. An
1322   IndexSel higher than IndexMax will be set to IndexMax by the PEA. An IndexSel lower than
1323   IndexMin will be set to IndexMin by the PEA.

       IndexCur        POL  PEA        DINT           Current Index Value
